# Practical Steps & Tips: Sharing the Gospel Naturally

> **A gentle word before we start.** This is *one* loving way to share Jesus, not the only way and not the "right" way. There's no script you have to nail and no quota to hit. The goal isn't to preach, perform, or win an argument. The goal is to **listen, ask, and love** the person in front of you. Relax. You're not closing a deal. You're starting a conversation and trusting God with the rest.
>
> *"Neither he who plants nor he who waters is anything, but only God who gives the growth."* (1 Corinthians 3:7)

---

## 🙏 Before You Go

Don't head out the door yet. This is spiritual work before it's anything else, so start on your knees.

**👉 Open the [Prayer Tab](#prayer) and pray Scripture first.** Ask God for *His* heart for the people you'll meet, so they feel loved and never like a project. Pray for open doors, soft hearts, and your own courage. Pray before, keep praying during, and pray after.

> *"It's not about ability, it's about availability."*

---

## Step 1 — A Friendly Approach

Lead with warmth. You're a neighbor, not a salesperson. Pick whichever opener feels natural to you.

**Opener A — The church invite:**

> "Hi, we're from a church down the street inviting people in the community to check it out. Do you go to church at all?"

- **If yes:** "That's great! What church? Is it a Christian or Catholic church?"
- **If no:** "Can I ask what your religious background is?"

**Opener B — The prayer offer:**

> "Hi, we're from a local church praying for our community. Is there anything I can pray for you, your health, or your family?"

- **Whether they say yes or no, gently follow up:** "May I ask what your religious background is?"

Either way, your job right now is simple: **find out where they are spiritually, and listen.** Let them talk more than you do.

---

## Step 2 — Deeper Questions to Open Hearts

Once you know a little about them, you can gently open the door to what matters most. This one question does a lot of work:

> "If something happened to you today, on a scale of 1 to 10, how certain are you that you'd go to heaven?"

Then, no matter their number, smile and ask:

> "Great — why do you say that?"

**Listen closely to their answer.** Most people will point to their own goodness ("I've tried to be a good person"). If so, you can gently reflect it back:

> "So it sounds like you'd say an 8 because you've done good things and been a pretty good person — is that right?"

That answer opens the door to the most important thing you can share: that none of us measures up on our own, but God has made a way.

> - We've **all** sinned and fall short of God's standard (Romans 3:23).
> - God is **just**, so He must punish sin — He can't just wink at it.
> - But God **loves** us, so He sent Jesus to pay for our sin and to credit *His* perfect righteousness to us. That's the only way any of us could ever meet God's standard.

Then hand it back to them gently:

> "What do you think about that?"

And if the moment is right:

> "Would you like to put your faith in Jesus, so you can be a 10 in your assurance?"

---

## Step 3 — Explaining the Difference (for Catholics & Other Faiths)

When someone comes from a Catholic background or another religion, **start with what you share**, then move to the one difference that changes everything. Be respectful and curious, never combative.

Begin with common ground:

> "Almost every religion actually agrees on two things: there's a standard, and we all fall short of it. Where they really differ is the *solution*."

Then draw the contrast gently:

> "Most every other religion says we get right with God by what *we* do — do this, do that, follow these steps. Christianity says the opposite: we *can't* do enough, so God did it for us. He sent Jesus to pay what we owe."

### The Judge Illustration

A simple story that helps it click:

> "Imagine someone murdered a person you love. Then at the trial, the man says, 'But judge, I've done a lot of good things in my life.' Would you want the judge to just let him go? Of course not — he has to be punished. Good deeds don't erase the crime. It's the same with us and God's law. Our good works, as nice as they are, can't pay the penalty for our sin."

### The Great Exchange

Then bring the hope:

> "Here's the beautiful part. On the cross, Jesus did two things. He took *our* sin and paid its full penalty. And He had lived the *perfect* life we never could — and He credits that perfect record to us. So by faith, He takes our sin and gives us His righteousness as a gift. That's a trade no other religion offers." (2 Corinthians 5:21)

---

## Step 4 — The One-Minute Gospel

Sometimes you just want a short, clear way to lay it all out. Here's a simple version (David Platt's):

> "God created us to know Him. But we've all turned away from Him — that's what sin is. God is holy and just, so He has to punish sin. But He loves us so much that He sent His Son, Jesus, to die on the cross and rise again three days later, paying for our sin and bringing us back to God. You can receive all of that right now, as a gift, by putting your faith in Jesus."

Keep the focus on God's love. Be honest about sin, but don't dwell there longer than you dwell on grace.

## Step 5 — Offering to Pray

Prayer is one of the most natural and loving things you can offer. Almost no one turns it down.

> "Could I pray for you right now, just for a moment?"

### Healing Prayer

If someone mentions a health issue, you can gently offer to pray for it on the spot:

1. Ask about the issue, and ask them to rate the pain from **1 to 10**.
2. Pray a simple, sincere prayer for healing.
3. **Retest:** "How does it feel now, on that same scale?"
4. If there's improvement, pray again, asking God to take it toward 0.

However it goes, leave them blessed. God is the healer; you're just the one who cared enough to ask.

## Step 6 — When They Make a Claim (The Columbo Tool)

Sometimes people will push back or make a big statement ("All religions are basically the same," "There's no way to know any of this is true"). You don't have to have every answer. Greg Koukl teaches a gentle approach built on **questions**, which keeps the burden of proof on the claim — not on you.

Just ask, with genuine curiosity:

> "What do you mean by that?"

(This helps you understand what they're *actually* saying.)

And then:

> "How did you come to that conclusion?"

(This gently invites them to explain *why* they believe it.)

You're not trying to trap anyone. Often your goal is simply to **"put a stone in their shoe"** — a thoughtful question they keep turning over later. Remember: lots of gardening (planting and watering), less harvesting. That's normal and good.

---

## Step 7 — Key Principles to Carry With You

- **Start with questions, then listen.** Find out where they are spiritually before you say anything else.
- **Speak the truth in love** — with humility, never superiority.
- **Ask God for His heart**, so people feel genuinely loved and never like a project.
- **Pray before, during, and after.** This is His work, not yours.
- **Be kind, especially when people aren't receptive.** A simple "Thank you, and Jesus loves you" can be its own seed.
- **Share the problem of sin, but keep the focus on God's love.**
- **You are not the Savior.** You plant or water; God gives the growth.

---

## Step 8 — A Final Word of Encouragement

If your hands are a little shaky, you're in good company. Every faithful witness has felt it. You don't need to be eloquent, and you don't need to have it all figured out. You just need to be *available* — willing to show up, ask a question, and love the person in front of you.

Some conversations will go long; many will be short. Some people will be warm; some won't. That's okay. **Success isn't a "yes" — success is faithfulness.** You showed up, you cared, you pointed to Jesus. Leave the results to the One who actually changes hearts.

> *"...for God loves a cheerful giver."* Go gently. Go prayerfully. Go in His love.
